🔄 Repentance

What Repentance Actually Is

← Back to Study Guides

Hook

Most people carry a definition of repentance that makes them want to avoid it. The word sounds like punishment, like groveling, like a long negotiation with a disappointed God. If that is your picture, the word itself becomes a barrier — something to get through rather than something to run toward.

But the word repentance, when you trace it back to its roots, means something closer to: come home. Or: think differently. Or simply: turn. It describes a reorientation — a change of direction at the level of the heart — not just a modification of behavior. Understanding what the word actually means changes what the experience actually feels like.

Deepen

The Hebrew: Shuv

The Old Testament word translated as "repent" is almost always shuv (שׁוּב). It means to turn, to return, to come back. It is the same word used when a traveler reverses course, when a prodigal goes home, when a people scattered in exile returns to the land.

When the prophets call Israel to repent, they are not calling them to feel worse about themselves. They are calling them to turn around and face a different direction. The repentance God asks for is a movement, not a mood.

This is why Ezekiel 18:30–32 is so striking: "Repent, and turn yourselves from all your transgressions... Cast away from you all your transgressions... and make you a new heart and a new spirit: for why will ye die, O house of Israel? For I have no pleasure in the death of him that dieth, saith the Lord God: wherefore turn yourselves, and live ye." The final word is live. Repentance is toward life, not away from it.

The Greek: Metanoia

The New Testament word is metanoia (μετάνοια). It is formed from meta (beyond, after, change) and nous (mind, understanding, inner life). Together: a change of mind, a transformation of inner perception.

This is not superficial. Metanoia is not "I will stop doing that thing." It is "I see differently now." The actions change because the person who is looking has changed. This is the reason Latter-day Saint theology insists that genuine repentance produces a mighty change — not willpower applied to behavior, but a new way of perceiving, wanting, and being.

When John the Baptist says "Bring forth therefore fruits meet for repentance" (Matthew 3:8), he is asking for evidence of metanoia — proof that the inner change has actually happened. Changed fruit grows from a changed root.

Alma 5 — The Diagnostic

Alma 5 is perhaps the most sustained treatment of what genuine repentance and spiritual change look like in all of scripture. Alma does not ask "Have you stopped sinning?" He asks a series of probing diagnostic questions: "Have ye experienced this mighty change in your hearts?" (v. 14). "Can ye look up to God at that day with a pure heart and clean hands?" (v. 19). "And now I ask of you, my brethren of the church, have ye spiritually been born of God? Have ye received his image in your countenances?" (v. 14).

The questions point inward. They are asking about a state of being, not a record of performance. Repentance, in Alma's framing, is the process by which God's image begins to appear in a person's life — not just the process of clearing a ledger.

Verse 26 is the one that lands: "And now behold, I say unto you, my brethren, if ye have experienced a change of heart, and if ye have felt to sing the song of redeeming love, I would ask, can ye feel so now?" Repentance is not a one-time event. It is an orientation that must be renewed — and the question of whether you can feel it now is always in play.

Cross-References

Read: Alma 5:14–26

The passage most useful for this study. Alma asks whether the mighty change has actually happened, and his questions reveal what genuine repentance produces in a person.

Read: Ezekiel 18:30–32

God's call to turn and live — the shuv framework in its most direct Old Testament form.

Read: Matthew 3:1–10

John the Baptist's preaching — "Bring forth therefore fruits meet for repentance" — the metanoia call and the demand for evidence of inner change.

Read: Ezekiel 36:26

"A new heart also will I give you, and a new spirit will I put within you." God promises that the transformation of repentance is something He does, not something we accomplish alone.

Conference References

Elder Dieter F. Uchtdorf — "You Can Do It Now!" (General Conference, October 2013):

Uchtdorf argued that repentance is one of the most beautiful concepts in the gospel because of what it actually means — a change of heart and direction, a return to God. He pushed back against the idea that repentance is something to dread or delay, framing it instead as an invitation that is always available and always answered.

You Can Do It Now! — Elder Dieter F. Uchtdorf, October 2013

Elder Dale G. Renlund — "Repentance: A Joyful Choice" (General Conference, October 2016):

Renlund directly challenged the cultural association of repentance with pain and shame, teaching that repentance is a gift — described in the Book of Mormon as one of God's most generous offerings. He emphasized that we are invited to repent, not condemned to it.

Repentance: A Joyful Choice — Elder Dale G. Renlund, October 2016

Elder D. Todd Christofferson — "The Divine Gift of Repentance" (General Conference, October 2011):

Christofferson grounded repentance in the principle that God loves us too much to leave us as we are. Repentance is not the consequence of divine disappointment — it is the vehicle of divine love. He drew a clear line between repentance as transformation and repentance as mere outward compliance.

The Divine Gift of Repentance — Elder D. Todd Christofferson, October 2011

President Russell M. Nelson — "We Can Do Better and Be Better" (General Conference, April 2019):

Nelson used strikingly direct language — "repent" without softening it — and presented repentance as something disciples do continually, not only in moments of serious moral failure. He connected repentance to the covenant path and to the ongoing work of becoming.

We Can Do Better and Be Better — President Russell M. Nelson, April 2019

Closing Question

The word repentance means turn — a reorientation, a coming home. If you set aside whatever weight the word carries for you and asked instead, "What direction am I currently facing, and is it toward God?" — what would your honest answer be? And what would one small turn look like today?
