📜 Patriarchal Blessings

How to Study Your Blessing

← Back to Study Guides

Hook

Most people read their patriarchal blessing a few times and put it away. They are waiting for something — maybe for a promise to come true, or for a confusing phrase to make sense. In the meantime, the blessing sits.

But the blessing is not a lottery ticket to check at the end of life. It is a living document, meant to be worked. This file is about how to do that.

Reading It as Scripture

The pattern of deep reading

Read: 2 Nephi 4:15–16

What to look for: Nephi writes that his soul "delighteth in the scriptures" and that he "pondereth" them and writes them for his children's learning. Notice the posture — delight, pondering, and intention. He doesn't just read; he works with the text.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Nephi says pondering is how the scriptures become useful. What does it look like to ponder your patriarchal blessing — not just read it?
  • He writes that his soul delights in the scriptures — that's an emotional response, not just an intellectual one. Has your blessing ever moved you emotionally? What did that feel like?
  • Nephi meditates on scriptures for his children's benefit. Who in your life might eventually benefit from what you learn through your blessing?

What "personal scripture" requires of you

Read: D&C 1:37–38

What to look for: The Lord says "what I the Lord have spoken, I have spoken, and I excuse not myself; and though the heavens and the earth pass away, my word shall not pass away." The standard for scripture — it endures. Your blessing was spoken by that same authority.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • If your blessing was given by priesthood authority and recorded faithfully, it carries the same weight as scripture in that verse. How does that change your obligation toward it?
  • D&C 1:38 says whether by his own voice or by the voice of his servants, it is the same. The patriarch is a servant. What does the word "same" mean for how you treat the patriarch's words?
  • Have you treated your blessing differently than you treat canonized scripture? What might shift if you treated them the same?

Practical Study Methods

Looking for patterns and themes

Read: True to the Faith: Patriarchal Blessings

What to look for: The entry encourages reading often, especially in times of decision or trial. It also notes that understanding deepens over time. This implies the blessing has layers — not everything is visible on the first read.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Read your blessing slowly and identify every distinct theme or category: gifts mentioned, callings mentioned, warnings mentioned, promises mentioned. What categories does it cover? Which surprise you?
  • Are there phrases that confuse you, or promises that feel far from where you are now? Write them down. What would you need to understand to receive those phrases?
  • True to the Faith says "the fulfillment of promises is often related to your faithfulness." Which promises in your blessing feel most dependent on something you need to do?

Looking for conditionals and absolutes

Read: D&C 82:10

What to look for: "I, the Lord, am bound when ye do what I say; but when ye do not what I say, ye have no promise." This is the framework for every conditional promise in your blessing. Some things in your blessing are conditional; some may be unconditional. Learning to tell the difference is a study skill.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Go through your blessing and mark every "if" or conditional phrase. What is God asking of you in exchange for these promises? Is that a good trade?
  • Are there promises in your blessing that seem unconditional — given without conditions? How do you hold those differently than the conditional ones?
  • Verse 10 says the Lord is "bound" by this covenant. What does it mean to have a binding promise from God — and what responsibility does that place on you?

Understanding what the blessing does not say

Read: Gospel Topics: Patriarchal Blessings

What to look for: The entry notes that "not all blessings contain all topics." A blessing may not mention marriage, or mission, or specific children. Silences are not denials — they are simply the limits of what was prompted in that moment.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Is there something absent from your blessing that you hoped would be there? How do you hold that absence — as a closed door, an open question, or something else?
  • The Spirit prompts what to say, not a template. What does it mean that your blessing is shaped by what God specifically chose to say to you, not what he says to everyone?
  • Could a blessing ever say the wrong thing — be mistaken? How does Church guidance handle that possibility? What does it ask of our trust?

Returning to It in New Seasons

The blessing as a lifelong companion

Read: 2 Nephi 32:3

What to look for: "Feast upon the words of Christ; for behold, the words of Christ will tell you all things what ye should do." The blessing is not the only source of revelation — but it is one of the most direct. Nephi says words of Christ tell you what to do. Your blessing can function that way.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • "Feast" is not the same as "snack." What does it mean to feast on your blessing — to really consume it and be nourished by it?
  • Has there been a time in your life when your blessing gave you clear direction about what to do? If not — have you asked it to?
  • Nephi says the words tell us "all things." That's a strong claim. How does a blessing that doesn't mention everything still function as a guide for all things?

Elder Scott — using revelation as a guide

Read: Richard G. Scott — Using the Supernal Gift of Prayer, April 2007 General Conference

What to look for: Scott describes how to record and preserve spiritual impressions — writing them, returning to them, letting them guide decisions. The principles apply directly to how to use a patriarchal blessing. Look especially for his counsel about reviewing recorded promptings in new circumstances.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Scott says written revelations become a resource across years of life. Have you treated your blessing that way — as a resource to consult at key moments?
  • He describes how returning to recorded impressions reveals new layers. When did you last read your blessing in a new life season? What did you notice that you hadn't before?
  • What would it look like to schedule regular reading of your blessing — not waiting until crisis, but as a regular spiritual practice?

Closing Question

Your blessing was given once. But you can receive it many times — each reading is a new encounter with who God sees when he looks at you.

Ponder or write:

Choose one phrase from your blessing that has always felt puzzling or distant. Spend time with it this week — research the words, pray about it, look for it in scripture. Write what you think it might mean, and what it might be asking of you.
