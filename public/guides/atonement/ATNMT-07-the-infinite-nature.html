The Infinite Nature of the Atonement

What "Infinite" Actually Means

The only scriptural use of "infinite Atonement"

Read: Alma 34:10–14

What to look for: Amulek argues that an infinite and eternal sacrifice is required — one that transcends the animal sacrifice system that could not permanently cleanse. Verse 10 contains the phrase "an infinite and eternal sacrifice." He then argues in verse 14 that this must be the sacrifice of a God. Only a God could make the sacrifice infinite.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Amulek says no ordinary man could make an atoning sacrifice that covers all humanity — it required a being whose life had infinite value. What does that tell you about who Jesus Christ must actually be?
  • He says the law of Moses was a "shadow" pointing to this infinite sacrifice (v. 14). What does the word "shadow" suggest about how the lesser thing related to the greater?
  • Verse 10: the Atonement must be infinite or it "cannot bring about the resurrection of the dead." Why is infinity a requirement of resurrection? Why does scale matter here?

Callister's systematic treatment of "infinite"

Read: The Infinite Atonement — Tad R. Callister (Deseret Book, 2000) — Chapters 2–4 cover the meanings of infinity in the Atonement.

This book is not available free online; it is available through Deseret Book and most LDS libraries. Search https://rsc.byu.edu for related RSC publications on the Atonement.

What to look for: Callister identifies at least five dimensions in which the Atonement is infinite: in the depth of suffering, in the breadth of coverage (all humanity), in the quality of the one who suffered (a God), in the duration of its effects (eternal), and in the categories of what it covers (sin, suffering, death, weakness). Each is a different axis of infinity.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Which of Callister's five dimensions of infinity is most surprising or most meaningful to you, and why?
  • He argues that the suffering was not merely symbolic or representative — it was the actual absorption of every individual's pain. Does that claim push against what you can easily believe? What would fully believing it do to your daily life?
  • Callister says the Atonement is infinite in quality partly because Christ's life had infinite worth as the Son of God. What does it mean for your sense of your own worth that the being of infinite worth suffered specifically for you?

The God who suffers — what infinity required

Read: None Were with Him — Jeffrey R. Holland, April 2009 General Conference

What to look for: Holland argues that for the Atonement to be infinite, Christ had to descend below all things — including the experience of divine abandonment. He had to go to a place no mortal has ever been: fully alone without God's presence. That is part of what makes it infinite in reach.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Holland says the Father withdrew so that Christ could know abandonment from the inside. No one who has ever felt abandoned by God is in a place Christ did not go first. How does that reframe your own darkest spiritual experiences?
  • Infinity in this framework means not just big — it means reaching the absolute bottom. What is the "absolute bottom" in your own life that you wonder if the Atonement reaches?
  • Holland says we can say to God in our hardest moments: "Thou knowest." How does Christ's infinite descent make that claim credible?

Infinite in Breadth — Every Person

Every soul matters — the lost sheep and coin

Read: Luke 15:1–10

What to look for: Jesus tells two parables in response to Pharisees who complain that he "receiveth sinners." A shepherd leaves 99 to find one. A woman lights a lamp and sweeps the whole house for one coin. In both parables, the search is comprehensive — no corner is left unsearched — and the finding produces a celebration disproportionate to the object found.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • The shepherd leaves 99 to find one. Does that arithmetic make sense to you — or does it feel reckless? What does it say about how God calculates individual worth?
  • The woman sweeps the whole house. What does total effort to find a single lost thing suggest about how thorough God's pursuit of you is?
  • Both parables end with celebration: "There is joy in the presence of the angels of God over one sinner that repenteth" (v. 10). What does it mean to you that your return produces joy in heaven?

The Atonement reaches every culture and condition

Read: Chieko Okazaki on the universal reach of the Atonement — BYU Women's Conference.

Search for Okazaki at https://womensconference.byu.edu/transcripts — her addresses from the early-to-mid 1990s consistently address the Atonement's reach across every culture and condition. Her book "Lighten Up!" also addresses this theme.

What to look for: Okazaki consistently insists that the Atonement is not culturally filtered — it reached Japanese Americans in internment camps, it reached Hawaiian fishing families, it reached women whose suffering looks nothing like the sanitized version in lesson manuals. Look for her insistence on the specific and the particular.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Okazaki argues against a generic or "one-size-fits-all" picture of the Atonement. In what specific ways does your experience of suffering, joy, or culture need to be seen as covered by the Atonement?
  • She speaks as a Japanese American who was incarcerated during World War II. How does her particular vantage point expand what "infinite" means when applied to the Atonement?
  • What would it mean for your daily faith if you truly believed Christ suffered specifically for the particular texture of your particular life — not a generic version of it?

The Atonement for those who never heard

Read: Gospel Topics: Salvation for the Dead

What to look for: The restored gospel's distinctive answer to the problem of those who died without hearing the gospel: the Atonement is infinite in reach across time, not only space. Proxy ordinances allow everyone who has ever lived to receive the benefit of what Christ did.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • The entry says God is perfectly just and perfectly merciful to every person who has ever lived. How does temple work — proxy ordinances for the dead — demonstrate what "infinite" means applied to the Atonement?
  • What does this doctrine say about who God is? What kind of God makes provisions for every person who ever lived, across all of human history?
  • If the Atonement reaches every person ever born, how does that affect how you think about ancestors, people of other faiths, or those who died in terrible circumstances?

Infinite in Depth — Every Kind of Pain

He descended below all things — D&C 88

Read: D&C 88:6

What to look for: "He that ascended up on high, as also he descended below all things, in that he comprehended all things, that he might be in all and through all things." The phrase "below all things" is used only once in scripture. Descending below all things is what made it possible for Christ to be present in all things.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • "Below all things" — what is below all things? What human experience is at the very bottom? Why did Christ need to go there?
  • The verse says the descent made it possible for him to "comprehend all things." How does suffering below all things produce comprehension of all things?
  • "That he might be in all and through all things" — he is present in suffering because he went there. Where in your life do you most need to know Christ is present with you?

Romans 8 — nothing can separate us from this love

Read: Romans 8:31–39

What to look for: Paul lists every category of terrible circumstance — tribulation, distress, persecution, famine, peril, sword — and then declares "in all these things we are more than conquerors." Then he extends the list beyond circumstances to cosmic forces: "neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor principalities... nor height, nor depth, nor any other creature, shall be able to separate us from the love of God."

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Paul's list keeps expanding — he lists more and more things that cannot separate us. What is on your personal list of things you fear might separate you from God? Does Paul include it?
  • "More than conquerors" — not just survivors, not just people who endure, but those who conquer through Christ. What is the difference between enduring and conquering?
  • Verse 34: Christ "maketh intercession for us." The risen Christ is still actively praying for you. How does that live reality — not a past event but a present intercession — change how you approach difficulty?

Infinite in Time — Past, Present, and Future

The Atonement was efficacious before it happened

Read: Moses 7:45–47 and Moses 5:6–8

What to look for: In Moses 5, Adam and Eve offer sacrifice after being expelled from Eden — centuries before Christ. An angel teaches Adam that the sacrifice is "a similitude of the sacrifice of the Only Begotten." The Atonement was retroactively effective — it covered people who lived before it happened, because it was planned before the earth was formed.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Adam and Eve were forgiven through an Atonement that had not yet happened. What does that tell you about the nature of God's commitments? He holds promises that transcend time.
  • Moses 7 records Enoch weeping, and God weeping, over human suffering. The Atonement was planned from the beginning but the suffering is still real and still mourned. How do you hold God's eternal plan alongside the reality that present suffering still matters to him?
  • The Atonement was infinite in time — past, present, and future. What does it mean to live now inside a covenant whose power reaches backward and forward beyond your own lifetime?

The infinite Atonement and eternal progression

Read: D&C 76:40–43

What to look for: The celestial vision describes Christ as the one "who glorifies the Father, and saves all the works of his hands, except those sons of perdition." The Atonement's infinite reach includes exaltation, glorification, and eternal life — not merely pardon from sin but an elevation to the "fulness of the Father."

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • The Atonement does not merely restore what was lost to the Fall — it elevates us beyond where we began. How does that trajectory — not just recovery but exaltation — change how you understand what Christ purchased?
  • "Saves all the works of his hands" — all of God's creation is the intended beneficiary of the Atonement. How large is your vision of what Christ's sacrifice accomplished?
  • Verse 43: the Atonement "glorifies the Father." The Son's sacrifice glorifies the Father — not the other way around. What does that reveal about the relationship between the Father and the Son in the plan of salvation?
