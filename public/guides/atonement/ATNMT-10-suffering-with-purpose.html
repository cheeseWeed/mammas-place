Suffering with Purpose: The Atonement Sanctifies, Not Just Removes

← Back to Study Guides

The Hook: Why Doesn't God Just Take It Away?

You have probably prayed — sincerely, faithfully — for a trial to end. And it didn't end. That is not a sign the Atonement failed you. But it raises a real question: if Christ has all power, and if the Atonement covers all things, why are faithful people still in pain?

This file works through what the scriptures and latter-day prophets actually teach: that the Atonement does not primarily function as a removal system. It functions as a sanctifying power — one that can transform what suffering does to you, not just whether you suffer. The difference between suffering that refines and suffering that merely hurts is rarely the suffering itself. It is what happens in the soul during it.

Deepening: Christ Went Lower Than Any of Us

"The Son of Man hath descended below them all"

Read: D&C 122:7–8

What to look for: The Lord speaks to Joseph Smith in Liberty Jail — one of the most desolate circumstances in Church history. He does not say "your suffering was a mistake" or "it will end soon." He says these things will "give thee experience, and shall be for thy good." Then he grounds that promise in a fact about Christ: "the Son of Man hath descended below them all." That descent is the basis for everything God says in this passage.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • The phrase "give thee experience" could feel dismissive — a polite way of saying "endure it." But read it again: God is saying that experience itself has value, that you will emerge knowing something you could not know without this. What experience have you gained from something painful that you would not have traded away in the end?
  • "Descended below them all" is a comparative claim: whatever you have faced or will face, Christ went lower. How does that truth change the feeling of being alone in your pain?
  • God says these things shall be "for thy good." Do you believe that about the hardest thing you are currently facing? If not, what would it take to begin to believe it?

The promise that precedes the pain

Read: D&C 121:7–8

What to look for: Just two chapters earlier, God makes the foundational promise to Joseph: "thine adversity and thine afflictions shall be but a small moment." This is the frame for D&C 122. The promise is not that the trial is trivial — Joseph's trial was not trivial. It is that eternity makes duration look different. Read verse 8 carefully: "if thou endure it well, God shall exalt thee on high."

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • "But a small moment" — is this a comfort or a challenge to you right now? What would it mean to hold your current suffering against the backdrop of eternity?
  • The promise is conditional: "if thou endure it well." Not just endure it — endure it well. What is the difference between enduring and enduring well?
  • God speaks to Joseph while he is still in Liberty Jail — the promise precedes the deliverance. Why does the timing matter? What does it mean to have God's promise before the ending has arrived?

Cross-References: What the Broader Canon Teaches

Tribulation as the path to hope — not a detour around it

Read: Romans 5:3–5

What to look for: Paul traces a chain: tribulation → patience → experience → hope. Notice he does not say tribulation leads to relief, which then produces hope. The tribulation itself is the first link in the chain. Verse 3 says we "glory in tribulations also" — and Paul gives a reason. Read carefully to find the mechanism.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Paul's chain ends in "hope" that "maketh not ashamed" because the love of God is poured into our hearts by the Holy Ghost. The suffering is not the point — what grows through the suffering is. What has grown in you through suffering that could not have grown any other way?
  • "Patience" is a middle link that tends to get skipped over. How long is the patience stage supposed to last? How do you sustain it when it stretches longer than you expected?
  • Paul says hope that does not shame us comes from knowing we are loved by God. When you are in the middle of a tribulation, what evidence reminds you that you are still loved?

Light and momentary — when it doesn't feel that way

Read: 2 Corinthians 4:17

What to look for: Paul describes troubles as "light and momentary" — but Paul's resume includes beatings, shipwreck, imprisonment, and being left for dead. He is not writing this from a comfortable life. The word translated "achieving" or "working" in some translations is key — the troubles are actively doing something. They are not passive. They are "working for us a far more exceeding and eternal weight of glory."

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Paul calls his very real suffering "light and momentary." This is not denial — it is a perspective produced by what he can see beyond it. What would it take for you to hold your hardest current difficulty as "light and momentary" in that same sense?
  • The troubles are described as "working" an "exceeding and eternal weight of glory." This implies a product. What is being produced in you right now by your current difficulty?
  • Read verse 18 alongside verse 17: "while we look not at the things which are seen, but at the things which are not seen." The perspective shift is deliberate — "while we look." How do you train yourself to look at the unseen rather than the seen when the seen is painful?

He knows how to succor — because He went there first

Read: Alma 7:11–12

What to look for: Alma says Christ took upon himself pains and sicknesses — not only sins. He took upon him the physical, the emotional, the afflicted. Verse 12 gives the reason: "that he may know according to the flesh how to succor his people according to their infirmities." The word "succor" means to run to the aid of. He knows how to run to you because he has already been where you are.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Alma distinguishes pains and sicknesses from sins — which means the Atonement covers things that are not your fault. What in your life right now is simply painful, not sinful — and how does this verse speak to that?
  • "According to the flesh" — his knowledge of your experience is embodied, not theoretical. He did not just observe suffering; he inhabited it. What difference does it make to you that Christ's knowledge of your pain is experiential rather than observational?
  • "How to succor his people according to their infirmities" — the succoring is tailored to the infirmity. He does not run to you with a generic response. How have you experienced that kind of specific, fitted help in a difficult time?

Even the Son of God learned through suffering

Read: Hebrews 5:8–9

What to look for: Verse 8 contains one of the most striking paradoxes in scripture: "Though he were a Son, yet learned he obedience by the things which he suffered." The Son of God — sinless, perfectly faithful — still had things to learn, and the mechanism of learning was suffering. Verse 9 says he was thereby "made perfect" and became "the author of eternal salvation."

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • If even Christ "learned obedience" through suffering, what does that say about suffering's role in the plan — not as a flaw in it, but as a feature of it? How does that reframe what you are going through?
  • "Though he were a Son" — the though carries weight. His divine identity did not exempt him from the learning process. How does knowing that change how you feel about not being exempted from yours?
  • He was "made perfect" through this process and became the author of salvation. The word "author" implies origination — he can write you into the story of salvation because he wrote it from suffering. How does his suffering-made-completeness qualify him uniquely to help you through yours?

Submissiveness as the turning point

Read: Mosiah 3:19

What to look for: King Benjamin identifies the natural man as an enemy to God — and lists what must replace natural-man patterns: the Saints yield to the enticings of the Holy Spirit and become "as a child, submissive, meek, humble, patient, full of love, willing to submit to all things which the Lord seeth fit to inflict upon him." That phrase — "all things which the Lord seeth fit to inflict" — is either troubling or transforming depending on what you believe about God's character.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • "Willing to submit to all things which the Lord seeth fit to inflict upon him." This is not passive suffering — it is an active, chosen posture of the will. What is the difference between resigned suffering and willing submission?
  • Benjamin lists submissiveness alongside meekness, humility, patience, and love — these are character traits, not just attitudes toward hardship. How might suffering, willingly submitted to, develop each of those traits in you?
  • Is there something you are currently resisting that you may be called to submit to? What would willing submission look like — not resignation, but trust?

Church History: The People Behind the Revelations

Liberty Jail — where D&C 121–122 came from

Read: Liberty Jail — Church History Topics

What to look for: Joseph Smith and five other Church leaders were imprisoned in Liberty, Missouri from December 1838 through April 1839 — four months. The conditions were brutal: thick limestone walls, straw on a dirt floor, coarse food they could not cook themselves, not enough blankets to stay warm. Joseph wrote in a letter: "We are kept under a strong guard, night and day… our food is scant, uniform, and coarse." It was in this specific dungeon that he cried out "O God, where art thou?" and received the revelations now in D&C 121 and 122 — including the words "thine adversity and thine afflictions shall be but a small moment." The jail later became known among Latter-day Saints as both "a prison and a temple."

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • The revelation "peace be unto thy soul" came not after Joseph was released but while he was still imprisoned. What does the timing tell you about when God's comfort typically arrives — before or after the trial ends?
  • Hyrum Smith met his infant son Joseph F. for the first time through the jail door. Even in one of the darkest moments in Church history, there were ordinary human moments — a father meeting his child. What does it mean that God did not remove the humanity from the suffering, only transformed it?
  • "A prison and a temple" — some of the most spiritually rich sections of the Doctrine and Covenants came out of Joseph's most confined circumstances. What does that pattern say about where revelation can reach you?

The handcart companies — faith that would not break

Read: Handcart Companies — Church History Topics

What to look for: In 1856, the Willie and Martin handcart companies were caught by early winter storms in present-day Wyoming. About 1,500 people were stranded on the plains — facing exposure, frostbite, and starvation. Nearly 250 died. Church members in Salt Lake City organized a rapid rescue, with more than 300 volunteers going out onto the plains to meet them, ultimately saving over 1,200 lives. Survivors who lived into old age were known for their unwavering faith — not despite what they had endured, but shaped by it.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • The handcart survivors are famous not for being broken by what they suffered but for being strengthened. Several left accounts testifying that those frozen plains were where they felt the presence of God most directly. How does suffering produce a kind of certainty that comfort cannot?
  • The rescue came late — after hundreds had already died. God did not prevent the tragedy. He sent help into the middle of it. How does the handcart story challenge a theology where God prevents all suffering for the faithful?
  • The suffering of those 250 who died is not erased by the faith of the survivors. How do you hold both truths — that faith held, and that people still died — without collapsing one into the other?

Talk Pointers: Where to Go Deeper

Maxwell on submitting the will as the last offering

Read: Neal A. Maxwell, "Consecrate Thy Performance," BYU Devotional, 2002.

Available at speeches.byu.edu — search for "Consecrate Thy Performance Maxwell"

What to look for: Near the middle of the talk, Maxwell develops the idea that the will is the one thing that is truly and uniquely ours to give — our intellect, our body, our time are all gifts of God, but the will is ours in a unique way. He argues that consecrating the will — surrendering it to God — is the last and most personal offering, and that suffering is often the crucible in which that surrender becomes possible.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Maxwell argues that the will is the most personal thing you can give God. What would it mean to surrender your will — not just your behavior — in your current difficulty?
  • He connects this to suffering because suffering is where we discover whether we trust God's character enough to let him govern outcomes. How much of your current resistance to a trial is really a question about whether you trust God?
  • If the will is the last offering, what have you already offered? What remains?

Maxwell on suffering as tuition

Read: Neal A. Maxwell, "Endure It Well," General Conference, April 1990.

Available at churchofjesuschrist.org

What to look for: In the section roughly midway through the talk where Maxwell addresses what suffering accomplishes, he uses the language of "tuition" — that suffering is the price of a certain kind of education. He argues that the lessons of the soul that matter most cannot be taught any other way. He contrasts enduring it well with merely getting through it.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Maxwell says enduring "it well" is different from merely outlasting something. What does "well" mean to you? What would it require of you in your current difficulty?
  • The tuition metaphor implies you are enrolled in something — that the suffering is not random but purposeful instruction. What do you think you are being taught right now?
  • He acknowledges that the curriculum is hard. What gives you confidence that the Teacher knows what he is doing?

Wirthlin on turning suffering around

Read: Joseph B. Wirthlin, "Come What May, and Love It," General Conference, October 2008.

Available at churchofjesuschrist.org

What to look for: Wirthlin offers four principles; the strongest section for this topic is his treatment of learning to "turn it around" — his principle that when something goes wrong, our response can transform the experience. He draws on his own personal losses and the losses of his wife, Frances. The section near the end where he talks about perspective and compensatory blessings is particularly worth slowing down for.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • "Come what may, and love it" — this is not a command to pretend it doesn't hurt. Wirthlin himself experienced profound grief. What does "love it" mean in that context?
  • He talks about compensatory blessings — gifts that come alongside hard things rather than removing them. Have you experienced compensatory blessings in a difficulty? What were they?
  • His tone is gentle, not demanding. He speaks as someone who has suffered, not as someone lecturing from outside it. How does the credibility of a witness who has been there change how you receive counsel?

Holland on what "give thee experience" actually means

Read: Jeffrey R. Holland, "Lessons from Liberty Jail," General Conference, October 2008.

Available at churchofjesuschrist.org

What to look for: About halfway through the talk, Holland reads D&C 122:7–8 and then unpacks what the phrase "give thee experience" means — not as a consolation prize, but as a declaration about the purpose of opposition in God's design. He notes that Liberty Jail became a place of revelation rather than just a place of punishment, and argues that our most confining circumstances can become our most spiritually productive ones.

After reading, discuss or ponder:

  • Holland says Liberty Jail was "a place of learning as well as a place of suffering." Can you think of a difficult circumstance in your life that became, in retrospect, a place of learning? What did you learn there?
  • He argues that some of the most important sections of the Doctrine and Covenants came out of a prison. What is being produced — spiritually, personally — in your current confined or painful circumstance?
  • Holland's central claim is that God did not fail Joseph by leaving him in jail — he was doing something in that jail that required the jail. What does that say about the trials God does not remove from your life?

Closing Question

After working through this file, sit with this:

The Atonement does not promise exemption from suffering. It promises meaning for suffering — and a Savior who has gone lower than any human experience, who knows how to run to your specific need, who learned obedience through his own suffering, and who is present in yours.

The question is not whether you will face hard things. The question is: what is happening in you while you face them?

What is one thing you are willing to submit — truly submit, not just endure — to God this week? And what do you believe he is making of it?
