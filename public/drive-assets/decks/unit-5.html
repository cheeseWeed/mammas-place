Unit 5

Sharing the Road

Motorcycles. Bikes. Trucks. Pedestrians. Work zones.

About 22 minutes. The last unit. You're almost done.

You're not alone out there

Every other unit was about you and your car. This one is about the eight other kinds of road user you'll meet on the way to school.

  • Knowing the rules for each one = you stop guessing
  • Most of these rules are about space and visibility
  • Almost all are MUST-know for the DLD test

Who counts as a "vulnerable user"?

SHOULD KNOW

Utah law uses this phrase for anyone on the road without a metal shell:

  • Pedestrians (including workers in the road)
  • Bicyclists and e-bike riders
  • Motorcyclists and moped riders
  • Skateboards, roller skates, motorized scooters
  • Wheelchairs, electric mobility devices
  • People on horseback or in a horse-drawn vehicle
  • Open-cab farm tractors

If you intentionally crowd or scare one of these people, that's a citation + points on your record.

How much space when passing a bike?

MUST KNOW — CANONICAL

3 feet minimum

That's the Utah rule. Not 2 feet. Not "comfortable distance." Three feet of clear air between the side of your car and the cyclist.

  • If the lane is too narrow, you can briefly cross a dashed yellow centerline to give the 3 ft (if oncoming is clear)
  • If yellow is solid or oncoming traffic is there — you wait

Quick check

Which of these is NOT considered a vulnerable user under Utah law?

Answer: A driver in a passenger car
Vulnerable users include pedestrians, bicyclists, e-bikes, mopeds, motorcycles, skateboarders, wheelchairs, persons riding animals, and farm tractors without enclosed shells. A regular car driver is NOT in this category. Handbook p. 83.

What does a bike lane look like?

SHOULD KNOW

Bike lanes are marked with solid white lines.

You may NOT stop, park, or drive in one. Three exceptions:

  • Entering or leaving an alley/driveway
  • Police directing official duty
  • True emergency

When is it never OK to pass a cyclist?

MUST KNOW

Even with 3 feet available, you may NOT pass a cyclist:

  • Within 100 feet of an intersection or railroad crossing
  • Within 100 feet of a bridge, viaduct, or tunnel with obstructed view
  • Cresting a hill or curve with obstructed view

Hold your spot, wait for clear road, then pass.

How are bicycle lanes marked on Utah roadways?

Answer: Solid white lines

When can a bike take the whole lane?

SHOULD KNOW

Bicycles are vehicles — same rights, same responsibilities as cars.

A cyclist can ride in the center of the lane when:

  • The lane is too narrow to share safely
  • They're avoiding hazards (gravel, glass, parked-car doors)
  • They're turning left

You don't get to honk. You wait, then pass with your 3 feet.

Want to ride a motorcycle? You need an M.

MUST KNOW — CANONICAL

To legally ride a motorcycle on Utah roads, you need an M endorsement printed on your driver license.

  • Class D license alone — not enough
  • You add the M after a separate motorcycle skills test (or by finishing an approved rider course)
  • Street motorcycles also require liability insurance

Quick check

Within how many feet of an intersection or railroad crossing is it illegal to pass a cyclist?

Answer: 100 feet
No-pass zones for cyclists include within 100 feet of an intersection, RR crossing, or obstructed bridge/viaduct/tunnel. Handbook p. 84.

Quick check

Utah law requires you to give a bicyclist at least how much space when passing?

Answer: 3 feet
Utah's 3-foot passing law applies whenever you pass a bicyclist or other vulnerable user. If the lane is too narrow, you may need to briefly use the adjacent lane. Handbook p. 84.

Under 21 on a motorcycle? DOT helmet, no exceptions.

MUST KNOW

Riders and passengers under 21 must wear a DOT-approved helmet.

  • A baseball cap doesn't count
  • A leather hat doesn't count
  • A bike helmet doesn't count — it must say DOT inside

Same rule applies to street-legal ATVs.

Handbook p.90  |  Zero Fatalities

Did that motorcycle just cut between cars? Probably legal.

SHOULD KNOW

Utah allows lane filtering — not the same as lane splitting — when ALL of these are true:

  • Road has 2 or more lanes going the same direction
  • Posted speed limit is 45 mph or less
  • The motorcycle is going 15 mph or less
  • The vehicles being passed are fully stopped
  • It can be done safely

Not allowed on freeway on-ramps.

Handbook p.91  |  Zero Fatalities

What if a light won't see a motorcycle?

SHOULD KNOW

Traffic signals use buried sensors that sometimes don't pick up small bikes. Utah's dead-red rule:

A motorcycle, moped, or bicycle rider who is 16 or older may proceed through a red light after waiting at least 90 seconds, IF:

  • No other vehicles or pedestrians are present
  • The path is clear

Cars don't get this rule. Only the small stuff.

Handbook p.91  |  Zero Fatalities

Marked or unmarked — pedestrians have right of way

MUST KNOW

Yield to every pedestrian at every crosswalk — even ones not painted on the road.

  • Stop before the crosswalk line so other drivers can see the person
  • Even with a green light, yield to pedestrians still in the crosswalk
  • Never pass another car that's stopped at a crosswalk — there might be a hidden walker

Handbook p.91–92  |  Zero Fatalities

White cane or guide dog = full STOP

MUST KNOW

This is the highest yield obligation in the entire handbook.

STOP

  • White cane or guide dog
  • Marked crosswalk, unmarked crosswalk, side street, anywhere
  • You don't slow — you stop, completely, until they finish crossing

Quick check

Which condition is REQUIRED for legal motorcycle lane filtering in Utah?

Answer: The motorcycle must travel at 15 mph or less
Lane filtering is legal only when: 2+ same-direction lanes, posted limit 45 mph or less (or off-ramp), motorcycle 15 mph or less, vehicle being passed is STOPPED, move is safe. Handbook p. 91.

Trucks have four blind spots — the right is the worst

MUST KNOW

  • Front: up to 20 feet ahead of the cab
  • Left side: alongside the cab, smaller
  • Right side: alongside cab AND running the full length of the trailer — biggest blind spot
  • Behind: up to 200 feet

Mirror test: if you can't see the truck driver in their side mirror, they can't see you.

Handbook p.88–89  |  Zero Fatalities

A loaded truck needs more than 400 feet to stop

MUST KNOW

At 55 mph:

  • Your car: about 130–140 feet to stop
  • A fully-loaded tractor-trailer: more than 400 feet

Why? Weight. Eighty thousand pounds takes way longer to slow than two thousand. The brakes are powerful but the inertia is enormous.

What this means for you: if you cut in front of a truck and brake-check it, the truck physically can't stop. Give space, don't pinch.

Handbook p.88  |  Zero Fatalities

Trucks turning right? Don't sneak up on their right.

MUST KNOW

Watch a truck make a right turn at an intersection — it will swing LEFT first, then sweep right.

The gap that opens on the right side of the trailer looks like a free lane. It isn't. The trailer is about to close that gap as it pivots.

  • That right side is also the truck's biggest blind spot
  • Stay behind the truck until the turn is complete
  • This is one of the most common car-truck crashes in Utah

Handbook p.88  |  Zero Fatalities

Passing a truck? Know when to pull back in.

SHOULD KNOW

  • Pass on the LEFT, always, completely
  • Don't linger alongside — you're in the blind spot
  • Don't pull back in until you can see the whole front of the truck in your rearview mirror
  • Stopped behind a truck on a hill? Leave extra room — it may stall or roll backward

Quick check (3 questions)

A large trucks blind spot extends how far in front of the cab and how far behind?

Answer: 20 feet in front, 200 feet behind
Truck no-zones extend up to 20 ft in front of the cab, on both sides especially alongside the cab, and up to 200 ft behind. Handbook p. 89.

At 55 mph, a fully loaded tractor-trailer may take how many feet to come to a complete stop?

Answer: More than 400 feet
A fully loaded tractor-trailer at 55 mph may take MORE than 400 feet to stop - about 1/3 longer than a football field. Handbook p. 88.

A semi-truck ahead of you is signaling a right turn and starts swinging LEFT first. What should you do?

Answer: Stay behind - trucks swing wide left to make a right turn
Trucks swing wide LEFT before a right turn - the gap on the right is the pivot zone, which will close on your car. The right side is also the trucks biggest blind spot. Stay behind. Handbook p. 88.

Speeding in a work zone = double the fine

MUST KNOW

Orange signs, orange cones, orange-vested workers — you're in a work zone.

  • Court must fine at least DOUBLE the regular speeding fine
  • Applies even if no workers are visible
  • Applies at 2 a.m. on a Sunday
  • Applies even if traffic is moving fine

Answer: An M endorsement on your DL
An M endorsement on your DL is required to operate a motorcycle on Utah highways. Handbook p. 90.

A flagger beats every other sign and signal

MUST KNOW

In a work zone, the person with the STOP/SLOW paddle overrides:

  • Traffic signals
  • Posted signs
  • Lane markings
  • What your GPS is saying

Green light + flagger waving STOP = you stop. The flagger wins.

Handbook p.59  |  Zero Fatalities

Move Over Law — the 90-day suspension rule

MUST KNOW

Approaching ANY stationary vehicle with flashing hazards or amber/red/blue lights:

  • Slow down AND
  • Change lanes to a non-adjacent lane if practical

Applies to police, fire, ambulance — AND tow trucks, road-side assistance, UDOT, anyone with flashers on.

Violation: 4-hour live classroom course within 90 days. Don't take the course? 90-day license suspension.

Handbook p.86  |  Zero Fatalities

Sirens behind you? Right side, stop.

MUST KNOW

Emergency vehicle approaching with lights and siren:

  • Pull immediately to the RIGHT
  • STOP completely until it passes
  • Do not follow within 500 feet of a fire vehicle responding to an alarm
  • Do not stop or park on the same block as one

If you're in the HOV/express lane, the express-lane rule lets you cross the double white to exit right — safely.

Handbook p.86  |  Zero Fatalities

Flashing lights at a school = slower limit, now

SHOULD KNOW

School-zone signs are the only fluorescent yellow-green pentagon shape on the road.

  • Flashing yellow lights = the slower posted limit is in effect
  • Crossing guards function like flaggers — obey them, period
  • Watch for kids on the sidewalk who might dart out

MUST KNOW

Now answer:

You're southbound on a 4-lane DIVIDED highway with a grass median. A school bus northbound on the other side of the median stops with red lights flashing. What do you do?

Answer: You're not required to stop — the median separates traffic, so only northbound (behind the bus) must stop
On a divided highway with a median (4+ lanes), only vehicles behind the bus must stop. A median or a center turn lane between you and the bus exempts the opposite direction. Handbook p. 46.

Trains always have right-of-way

MUST KNOW

Trains have the right-of-way 100% of the time — over ambulances, fire engines, cars, police, pedestrians.

  • A freight train at 55 mph needs more than a mile to stop (~18 football fields)
  • TRAX light rail goes from 0 to 65 mph in 21 seconds. Don't try to beat it.
  • Never stop on tracks. Never pass another vehicle at a crossing.
  • Stop 15 to 50 feet from the nearest rail when signals or gates are active

Handbook p.95–97  |  Zero Fatalities

Class D covers ATVs and UTVs (if they're street-legal)

SHOULD KNOW

Your regular Class D Utah license is enough to drive a street-legal ATV, UTV, or full-size vehicle on a public road. No M endorsement needed.

  • "Street-legal" means it meets equipment requirements (lights, horn, mirrors, etc.)
  • Same DOT helmet rule under 21
  • If it's not street-legal, you can ride it on dedicated trails only

A school bus ahead has its alternating flashing RED lights on and the stop arm extended. You must:

Answer: Stop immediately before reaching the bus
Alternating flashing red lights + stop arm = STOP immediately before reaching the bus. Handbook p. 46.

If a police car pulls you over: here's the move

SHOULD KNOW

  1. Signal, move to the RIGHT shoulder (even from the HOV lane)
  2. Put the car in PARK and turn off the engine
  3. Stay in the vehicle, roll down your window
  4. Keep your seat belt on. Ask passengers to keep theirs on.
  5. Hands on top of the steering wheel
  6. For documents, keep one hand on the wheel and reach slowly

Don't get out of the car unless the officer asks you to.

Scenario 1: The narrow lane

You're on a 2-lane residential street. A cyclist is in your lane on the right. The lane is too narrow to give 3 feet without crossing the centerline. The yellow line on your side is dashed and no one is coming. You're not near an intersection.

What do you do?

Cross the dashed yellow briefly to give your 3 feet, then return. That's the legal move. If the line were solid, or oncoming traffic were there, you'd wait and follow.

Tests: pass-bicyclist-3-feet-minimum · dashed-yellow-passing · bicycle-no-pass-100-ft

Scenario 2: The truck's "free" gap

A tractor-trailer ahead signals a right turn. You're also turning right. You see a gap on the trailer's right side and think you can slip through.

Smart or not?

Bad move. The truck swings LEFT before swinging right — that gap is the pivot zone, and it's about to close around your car. The right side is also the truck's biggest blind spot. The driver doesn't see you. Stay behind.

Tests: truck-swings-left-for-right-turn · truck-largest-blind-spot-right

Scenario 3: The motorcycle that "cut in line"

You're stopped at a red light on State Street (40 mph posted). A motorcycle slides between your lane and the next one, going about 10 mph, and pulls up to the front of the line.

Was that illegal?

No — that's legal lane filtering. The conditions match: 2+ lanes same direction, speed limit at or under 45 mph, motorcycle under 15 mph, vehicles being passed are fully stopped. This is NOT the same as illegal lane splitting (which is between moving traffic).

Tests: motorcycle-lane-filter-conditions

Scenario 4: The tow truck on the shoulder

You're on a 4-lane highway. A tow truck is on the right shoulder with amber lights flashing, hooking up to a stalled car. There's no police presence.

Move Over Law applies?

Yes. Move Over applies to ANY stationary vehicle with flashing hazards or amber lights — not just police/fire/ambulance. Slow down AND change to a non-adjacent lane if practical. Skip it and you risk a 90-day suspension.

Tests: move-over-law · move-over-violation-90-day-suspend

Scenario 5: School bus across the median

You're driving NORTH on a 4-lane divided highway with a wide grass median. A school bus SOUTHBOUND on the opposite side of the median stops with red lights flashing.

Do you stop?

No. Because there's a physical median, only same-direction (southbound) vehicles must stop. You proceed with caution. If the median weren't there — both directions stop.

Weird Utah Law of the Day

The 14-Inch Wheel Rule

Utah's bicycle code (UCA §41-6a-1102) only counts a device as a "bicycle" if its wheels are 14 inches or more in diameter.

Why does that matter?

  • A kid's tiny BMX or push-bike with 12-inch wheels — technically not a bicycle under state law
  • It's treated more like a toy than a road vehicle
  • The 3-foot passing rule still applies to the human, but the bike-lane rules don't

Bonus oddity: Utah was the first state to legalize motorcycle lane filtering (2019). Most states still don't allow it.

The seven MUSTs of Unit 5

  1. 3 feet — minimum passing distance for any cyclist
  2. 100 feet — no passing a cyclist near intersections or RR crossings
  3. M endorsement — required to ride a motorcycle
  4. DOT helmet under 21 — rider AND passenger
  5. White cane = full STOP — highest yield obligation
  6. 400 feet — truck stopping distance at 55 mph
  7. Move Over Law — slow + change lanes for ANY flashing-light vehicle, or 90-day suspension
