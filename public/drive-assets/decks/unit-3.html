One sentence to live by

Right-of-way is something you give, not take.

Never assume you have it. When in doubt - yield.

Who goes first? The priority ladder

When two rules collide, the higher tier wins. Memorize this and almost every who-goes-first question answers itself.

The 15-tier ladder

  1. TRAINS - beat everything, even ambulances
  2. Emergency vehicles (lights + siren)
  3. Police officer directing traffic
  4. Flagger in a work zone
  5. School bus with flashing reds
  6. Pedestrians (white cane = full stop)
  7. Cars already in the intersection
  8. Oncoming traffic when YOU are turning left
  9. Car on your right at a 4-way stop tie
  10. Car on your right when signals are out
  11. Traffic already in a roundabout
  12. Traffic already on the interstate
  13. Street traffic when you are leaving a driveway
  14. First driver to a narrow/one-lane bridge
  15. Uphill driver on a mountain road (downhill yields)

Answer: The car on the street — driveway traffic yields

Trains always win

A 100-car freight train at 55 mph needs more than a mile to stop. That is ~18 football fields.

Trains have right-of-way over ambulances, fire engines, police, cars, and pedestrians - 100% of the time. Verbatim from the handbook.

Flashing lights at a crossing? Stop. Gates coming down? Stop. NEVER drive around lowered gates.

TRAX is a train too

Salt Lake City light rail follows the same crossing rules as freight trains.

TRAX trains hit 65 mph in 21 seconds. You will not beat one.

Stop. Wait for the gates to fully raise AND lights to stop flashing before crossing.

Sirens behind you? Pull right, stop.

Emergency vehicle with lights and/or siren approaching:

  1. Drive immediately to the RIGHT side of the road
  2. STOP until it passes

If you are stopped at a red light and a siren comes up behind you, DO NOT run the red. Pull right if you can. Stay put if you cannot.

Do NOT follow within 500 feet of a responding fire truck.

You stop at a railroad crossing — gates down, lights flashing, train approaching. An ambulance with lights and siren pulls up behind you. What do you do?

Answer: Wait — trains have the right-of-way 100% of the time, even over emergency vehicles. The ambulance must wait too
Trains have the right-of-way over ambulances, fire engines, cars, police, and pedestrians. Never drive around lowered gates. Handbook p. 96.

Move Over Law - for ANY stopped car

See a car stopped on the shoulder with flashing hazard lights? Slow down AND change lanes (to a lane not adjacent) if safe.

This applies to any stationary vehicle with hazards - not just emergency vehicles. Tow trucks, broken-down sedans, all of them.

Violation = 4-hour live classroom defensive driving course within 90 days. Skip the class = 90-day license suspension.

When a cop directs traffic

Officer directions override the traffic signals.

Green light + officer holding up a hand = STOP. Red light + officer waving you through = go.

Same logic applies to flaggers in work zones (next slide).

Quick check (2 questions)

You're stopped at a red light. An ambulance with lights and siren comes up behind you. What do you do?

Answer: Pull to the right when safe and stop; clear the intersection cautiously only if you can do so safely
Yield to emergency vehicles by pulling to the right and stopping. If you're at an intersection, clear it carefully — don't run a red light unsafely. Handbook p. 86.

On a 4-lane road, a regular passenger car is stopped on the right shoulder with hazard lights flashing. It's NOT an emergency vehicle. What must you do?

Answer: Slow down and change lanes away from the stopped vehicle if practical
The Move Over Law applies to ANY stationary vehicle with hazards — not just emergency vehicles. Slow and change lanes if practical. Handbook p. 86.

Flaggers beat signs and signals

In a work zone, a flagger with a STOP/SLOW paddle overrides every sign and signal in the zone.

Green light? Does not matter. Posted speed? Does not matter. Do what the flagger tells you.

Now answer:

On a 2-lane road, a school bus traveling the opposite way stops with its red lights flashing. What must you do?

Answer: Stop — on a 2-lane road, BOTH directions must stop
On a 2-lane road, traffic in both directions stops for a school bus with flashing red lights. Same rule on a 4-lane road with no median. A median or a center turn lane is the ONLY thing that exempts the opposite direction. Handbook p. 46.

Pedestrians at crosswalks

Yield to ALL pedestrians - entering or in the crosswalk - marked OR unmarked.

Yes, even if you have a green light. Yes, even if there are no painted lines.

Never pass a vehicle stopped at a crosswalk - there may be a pedestrian you cannot see.

White cane or guide dog

FULL STOP. No exceptions.

White cane or guide dog means a blind pedestrian. They get a complete stop regardless of crosswalk markings, regardless of your signal.

Implied crosswalks (no lines)

Every intersection of two streets has a crosswalk on each leg - even if there are no painted lines.

That sidewalk corner where it would naturally cross? That is the unmarked crosswalk. Pedestrians have right-of-way there.

4-way stop priority

  1. First to arrive goes first.
  2. Tie? Yield to the car on your RIGHT.
  3. Going straight beats turning across traffic.

If two cars face each other and one is turning left, the one going straight goes first.

Now answer:

In a diverging diamond interchange (DDI), what is unusual about the traffic flow on the bridge over the freeway?

Answer: Traffic briefly crosses over so you drive on the LEFT side of the road across the bridge
The DDI swaps traffic to the left side over the bridge so left turns onto the freeway don't have to cross oncoming traffic. Follow the lane markings and arrows — it feels strange but it's safer.

Quick check

A pedestrian carrying a white cane begins to cross a side street as you approach. There's no marked crosswalk. What must you do?

Answer: Come to a complete stop — the white cane requires a full stop regardless of crosswalk markings
A pedestrian with a white cane (or guide dog) requires a full STOP, marked crosswalk or not. Handbook p. 91.

What yellow really means

Yellow = the light is about to turn red. Stop if you can safely.

Yellow does NOT mean speed up to beat the red. That is a ticket. And, often, a crash.

Red, green, and right-on-red

  • Red - full stop behind the line, crosswalk, or intersection.
  • Green - go IF the way is clear (yield to pedestrians and cars still in the intersection).
  • Right on red - OK after a complete stop, unless a sign says No Turn on Red.

A right-on-red rolling stop is the cheapest ticket cops write. Come to a real stop.

Now answer:

What does a FLASHING YELLOW ARROW mean at a left-turn signal?

Answer: Turn allowed — but yield to oncoming traffic and pedestrians
Flashing yellow arrow = turn permitted, but you have NO priority. Yield to oncoming traffic AND pedestrians before turning. Handbook p. 56.

Left turn on green = YIELD

A solid green light gives you permission to enter the intersection, NOT priority over oncoming.

Yield to all oncoming traffic before you turn left. Only a green arrow gives you priority.

Stranded by a red left arrow that never changes? If safe and you are turning from a one-way onto a one-way, you may proceed.

At a red light, you want to turn right. What's the rule?

Answer: Allowed after a complete stop, unless a sign prohibits it
Right on red is allowed after a complete stop, unless posted otherwise. Handbook p. 56.

A steady YELLOW light means:

Answer: Stop if you can do so safely — the light is about to turn red
Yellow = light is about to change to red. Stop if you can safely. Handbook p. 55.

Roundabouts

  1. Slow down on approach
  2. Yield to traffic already in the circle AND pedestrians at the entry
  3. Enter when clear - flow is counterclockwise
  4. Signal your intent to exit

Roundabouts are NOT 4-way stops. Do not stop unless you have to.

Quick check

What does a red sign or red circle-with-slash on a sign generally mean?

Answer: Stop, yield, or prohibition
Red on a sign means STOP, YIELD, or PROHIBITION. A red circle with a slash means you cannot do the action shown.

On-ramps yield

You are on the ramp. Interstate traffic has the right-of-way.

Use the acceleration lane to match the speed of traffic, find a gap, and merge smoothly. If there is no gap and the ramp is ending, you may need to stop.

Interstate drivers are NOT required to move over for you - though many will if they can.

Driveways yield to the street

Coming out of a driveway or private road? You yield to all traffic on the public road.

This includes the sidewalk - yield to pedestrians and cyclists too. Full stop before the sidewalk.

Narrow bridges & one-lane roads

On a rural narrow or single-lane bridge, the first driver to the bridge has the right-of-way.

Take turns. Be polite. Out in the country this often becomes a friendly wave.

Mountain roads - downhill yields

Two cars meet on a narrow mountain road, one going uphill, one going downhill. Who backs up?

The downhill driver yields.

Counterintuitive - gravity does NOT win. Reason: uphill drivers have less control restarting from a stop on a grade.

School zones - when 20 is the law

Lights flashing on the school zone sign = 20 mph, period. Even if you do not see any kids.

Also 20 mph during school opening/closing hours and recess, lights or not.

Work zones - double fines, flaggers rule

  • Orange signs = work zone ahead
  • Speeding fines are DOUBLED in work zones
  • Slow down even if you do not see any workers - that is the law
  • The flagger paddle beats every sign and signal in the zone

Drivers, not workers, are the ones most often hurt in work zones. Slow down at the first cone — not at the last one.

Weird Utah moment

Trains beat ambulances. By law. In writing.

The Utah handbook spells it out: Trains have the right-of-way 100% of the time over ambulances, fire engines, cars, police, and pedestrians.

Most state handbooks dance around this. Utah just says it. A train carrying 100 cars at 55 mph needs more than a mile to stop - it physically cannot yield. So everyone else does.

Bonus: TRAX light rail counts as a train. Treat it the same.

Top 7 right-of-way MUSTs

  1. Trains beat everything - even ambulances
  2. Sirens behind you = pull right, stop
  3. School bus reds = stop. Median? Opposite direction is exempt
  4. Yield to all pedestrians, marked or unmarked. White cane = full stop
  5. 4-way stop tie = yield to the right
  6. Left turn on solid green = yield to oncoming
  7. Yellow = stop if safe. NEVER speed up
